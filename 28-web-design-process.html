Web Development Process

Six Stages

  1. Site definition & planning
  2. Information architecture
  3. Site design
  4. Site construction
  5. * Site Marketing
  6. * Tracking, evaluation, maintenance

* outside the scope of our course **

** unless you really want to know

Why a process?

The deeper one gets into the construction process, mistakes and changes become more costly and time consuming.

Site definition & planning

  • define your objectives
  • collect, analyze information you need to build the site

Document your plans

Don't go off memory. A written document becomes your 'blueprint' as you get deeper into each iteration. It helps you from forgetting a detail.

Information architecture

  • inventory your content
  • define the structure of your site
    • develop a site map
    • sketch wireframes
    • take measurements into account
    • solicit early feedback from potential users
    • establish a style guide (colors, fonts, etc.)
  • develop mockups or functional prototypes

Functional Prototypes

Photoshop/Illustrator/InDesign mockups only account for static design.

Functional prototypes account for interactivity.

Think Small

A functional prototype can be very detailed, but think of it as a way to test small aspects of your site. Build small examples of how you expect your site to 'respond.'

Think Small: Navigation

  • What is the default state?
  • What is the hover/focus state?
  • What happens when a user activates a link?
  • What response does interaction elicit?

Site Design

  • make sure all content is acquired, edited and proofread
  • graphical elements are sized based on wireframe specs
  • repeating design elements are are consistent (i.e. header, nav, footer)
  • behavioral specifications are defined

Everything should be polished.

Site Construction

Focus on:

  1. Structure
  2. Presentation
  3. Interactivity

If your site warrants it, develop a template.

Create a template

You may find that as you design your pages, a pattern will develop. Elements you should expect to be the same across pages:

  • header
  • navigation
  • containing element (maybe)
  • footer

Tip: Build one page with each of these elements 100 percent polished, then just copy the template!

Testing

As you finish each aspect of your site (structure, presentation), be sure to test your code.

Run your code through a validator. Make sure any tweaks you make are also valid.

Does the site appear as you expect it to?

Does the site function as expected?

When all tests check out, that's when you can deploy your site.
