Open Source & Web Ethics

What is open source?

  • closed source code is a "wasteful distribution of resources"
  • supports opening, modification and redistribution of code, software
  • encourages community contributions
  • introduced the principle of "copyleft"
    • using copyright law to ensure openness
    • choosealicense.com

History of Open Source Software

  • Bell Labs, AT&T's R&D arm, distributes UNIX to universities
  • Professors, students at University of California-Berkley create a fork of the software, BSD UNIX
  • DARPA funds BSD to provide a version of UNIX with networking capabilities for ARPANET
  • AT&T demands licensing fees of $200,000 for UNIX
    • 1992: UNIX System Laboratories files lawsuit against the UC Berkeley over use of proprietary source code
    • 1993: Out-of-court settlement, largely favors UC Berkeley
  • BSD and its code lives on in several operating systems today

Linux

  • a UNIX-like open-source operating system
  • created by Linus Torvalds in 1991
  • developed out of USL-UC Berkeley lawsuit which halted progress on BSD UNIX
  • further improvements facilitated by community contributions
  • revealed the strength of 'bottom-up' design
    • Linus' Law: Given enough eyeballs, all bugs are shallow
  • widely used, from traditional computers to embedded and mobile devices

Web Ethics

We can get a lot of great software, code, content for our site from the web.

DO NOT assume anything is for the taking.

Remember it is improper to:

  • link to images, scripts, etc. not hosted on your own server
  • use the code or design of another site as your own
  • use copyrighted photos, graphics, multimedia, etc. without permission
  • use proprietary source code

Public Domain

  • when intellectual property rights have expired (or are waived)
  • laws vary by country
  • works of the U.S. government are automatically released into public domain
    • work prepared by an "officer or employee" of the federal government
    • some items (i.e. logos) may still be protected by trademark laws

Fair use

  • sometimes copyrighted works may be used under 'fair use' law
  • does not require permission of copyright holders
  • conditions include (but not limited to):
    • commentary
    • criticism or parody
    • news reporting
    • research
    • teaching
    • library archiving
    • scholarship

Fair use test

17 U.S.C. § 107

  1. the purpose and character of the use, including whether such use is of a commercial nature or is for nonprofit educational purposes
  2. the nature of the copyrighted work
  3. the amount and substantiality of the portion used in relation to the copyrighted work as a whole
  4. the effect of the use upon the potential market for or value of the copyrighted work
