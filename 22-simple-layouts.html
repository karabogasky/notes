User-Friendly Design

Studies show users are most comfortable when reading a page with 60-70 characters across.

Based on that logic (and a practice inherited from designing to standard monitor sizes), we try not to expand a container beyond 960 pixels.

Middle Ground

Designing for a container 960 pixels wide also gives us a good starting point for any digital design.

Consider:

  • One of the largest monitors available today (Apple 30" Cinema Display) has a display width of 2560 pixels.
  • One of the smallest mobile devices available today (iPhone) has a display width of 320 pixels.

Assuming a design is not responsive, it is still possible to look good on both these extremes.

Stretching the Limits

There is nothing wrong with designing for bigger screens. Remember to maintain usability through responsive design.

  • never stretch readable content beyond the 60-70 character limit
  • bigger monitor, bigger fonts
  • scale down for smaller screens

A centered layout

One of the easiest layouts in web design is to take well-structured content, place it in a container and center the container.

You need two components to create a simple, centered layout:

  1. A div wrapping your content in your HTML file
  2. CSS declarations on the div specifying width and setting the left and right margin to auto.

Working with max-width

What if we wanted our centered container's content to adjust as it became smaller? (Maybe someone is viewing the site on a phone or tablet.)

When we use the max-width property, we're telling the browser to not let our content stretch beyond the value specified.

This provides us with flexibility to set space for our content in a design, but also make it elastic if the viewport is smaller than the allotted space.

Working with min-width

Some browsers (Firefox) will crush a block element without mercy.

What if our design called for flexibility, but not at the total expense of our content?

Using min-width will limit how much an element can be re-sized.
